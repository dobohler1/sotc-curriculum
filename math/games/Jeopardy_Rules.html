SOTC MATH JEOPARDY

Official Rules & Game Management Guide
BOTH ANSWERS MUST BE CORRECT TO SCORE
How It Works: Every question has two parts — a Tier A problem for younger players and a Tier B problem for older players. Your team must get BOTH right to earn the points. One right and one wrong = zero points. This means your whole team matters.
1 Teams

Four teams compete. Teams should be mixed age — every team needs younger AND older players.

RED TEAM BLUE TEAM GOLD TEAM GREEN TEAM
Why mixed age? Tier A questions are for younger kids. Tier B questions are for older kids. If your team has no younger players, nobody can answer Tier A — and you need both to score.
2 How Each Round Works
  1. Game Master picks a question (or lets the winning team pick). The board shows a category and point value.
  2. Both Tier A and Tier B appear on screen. The Game Master reads them aloud.
  3. All 4 teams huddle (60 seconds). Each team decides their Tier A answer and Tier B answer. Teams write BOTH answers on their whiteboard — one on top, one on bottom.
  4. "Boards up!" — All teams hold up their whiteboards at the same time. No changing answers after boards go up.
  5. Game Master reveals the correct answers and scores each team.

Multiple teams can score! Unlike TV Jeopardy, ALL teams answer every question. If 3 teams get both tiers right, all 3 teams earn the points. This keeps everyone engaged — there's no "sitting out" while another team answers.
Daily Double: When a Daily Double is revealed, the team that selected it can wager up to their current score (minimum 100 points). Only that team answers. Both tiers must still be correct to earn the wager — if either is wrong, they lose the wagered points.

MANAGING TEAMS

Handling Late Arrivals, Team Balance & Flexible Rosters
4 Initial Team Setup
  1. Sort students into two groups: "Younger" (roughly grades 1-3 or anyone working on Spark/Nebula/Drift warm-ups) and "Older" (roughly grades 4-7 or Helix/Blaze/Quantum/Cipher warm-ups).
  2. Count each group. Divide each group by 4 — that's how many of each go to each team.
  3. Assign using a snake draft: Alternate assigning younger/older students to Red, Blue, Gold, Green so teams stay balanced.
  4. Minimum requirement: Each team MUST have at least 1 younger AND 1 older player. If you don't have enough of one group, have a flexible player cover both tiers (see below).
Example: 24 kids arrive on time — 10 younger, 14 older.
Each team gets: 2-3 younger + 3-4 older = 6 per team.

Rules for Late Arrivals
  • Always join the smallest team. If two teams are tied for smallest, join whichever needs that age group more.
  • No switching teams. Once assigned, you're on that team for the whole game.
  • New players join between questions. Don't interrupt a huddle — wait until the current question is scored, then add the player.
  • The Game Assistant handles all team assignments so the Game Master can keep the game flowing. (See Roles below.)
Quick balance check: After every 3-4 questions, glance at team sizes. If one team has 2+ more players than another, consider re-balancing by moving the most recent addition. Only do this between questions.
6 Edge Cases
Situation Solution
A team has NO younger players Assign the youngest older player as a "flex" — they answer Tier A. Move the next younger late arrival to this team first.
A team has NO older players Assign the oldest younger player as a "flex" — they attempt Tier B. Move the next older late arrival to this team first.
Only 8-12 kids at start Run 2 teams instead of 4. Split into Red vs. Blue. When more kids arrive, split into 4 teams at the next natural break.
A student needs to leave early They quietly leave. Don't rebalance unless it creates a team with zero younger or zero older players.
Way more kids than expected (40+) Teams of 10+ are fine. Rotate who writes on the whiteboard each question so everyone stays involved.

Key rule: A younger team member must write the Tier A answer. An older team member must write the Tier B answer. This ensures the right kids are doing the right work — no older kid carrying the whole board.
8 Reviewing Answers
  1. "Boards up!" — All 4 teams raise their whiteboards simultaneously. No more writing after this call.
  2. Game Master clicks "Show Answer" on the jeopardy screen. The correct answers for both tiers appear.
  3. Game Master reads Tier A answer aloud. Scan all 4 boards. Announce which teams got Tier A correct.
  4. Game Master reads Tier B answer aloud. Scan all 4 boards. Announce which teams got Tier B correct.
  5. Award points ONLY to teams that got BOTH right. Click the + button for each scoring team on the game screen.
  6. Quick teach (optional): If most teams missed it, take 30 seconds to explain the answer. Keep it brief.
Acceptable Answers
SituationRule
Answer is mathematically correct but written differentlyAccept it. "1/2" and "0.5" are both correct. "3 remainder 5" and "3 R 5" are both fine.
Minor spelling error but answer is clearAccept it. We're testing math, not spelling.
Answer is close but not exact (e.g., "about 47" when answer is 46.8)Reject. Math must be precise. Exception: if the question said "round" or "estimate."
Work is shown but final answer is wrongReject. The final answer matters. Good effort though.
Team disputes a rulingGame Master's call is final. Keep the game moving. If legitimately unclear, give the benefit of the doubt.
9 Game Roles
RoleWhoResponsibilities
Game Master Lead Instructor Runs the board, reads questions, calls "boards up," reviews answers, awards points, keeps energy high. Controls the laptop.
Game Assistant Co-Instructor or Volunteer Handles late arrivals (assigns to teams), watches for cheating (phones, peeking), manages whiteboards/markers, does quick balance checks.
Team Captain 1 older student per team Leads the huddle, makes sure younger players answer Tier A, organizes who writes on the board. Keeps team focused.
Board Writer Rotates each question Writes the team's answers on the whiteboard. Younger player writes Tier A, older player writes Tier B.

10 Game Timeline (30-40 Minutes)

Pacing tip: If the game is dragging, skip to higher-value questions (more exciting). If energy is high, let the winning team pick the next question. Average about 60-90 seconds per question including huddle time.
11 Materials Checklist
  • ☐ Laptop or tablet with jeopardy game open in browser
  • ☐ Projector or large screen (so all 40 kids can see)
  • ☐ 4 whiteboards (or large paper/cardboard) — one per team
  • ☐ 8 dry-erase markers (2 per team — different colors help)
  • ☐ 4 erasers or paper towels
  • ☐ Team color indicators (colored tape, bandanas, or sticky labels)
  • ☐ This rules sheet (for Game Master reference)
  • ☐ Roster sheet (next page) for tracking team membership
  • ☐ Prizes (optional — stickers, snacks, bragging rights)
12 Pro Tips
Keep it loud and fun. This is a game, not a test. Cheer when teams get it right. Groan dramatically when they're close. Ham it up. Energy from the Game Master drives energy from the kids.
Rotate board writers. Tell Team Captains: "Pick someone different to write each question." This prevents one kid from dominating and keeps everyone involved.
Use the timer strategically. The game has a 60-second timer built in. For easier questions (100-200 pts), give 30-45 seconds. For hard ones (400-500 pts), give the full 60. Pause the timer if you need to re-read a question.
Connect to coding class. When a problem involves patterns, logic, or step-by-step thinking, point it out: "That's exactly how you think when you write code — break it into steps!" Reinforce why math matters for programming.
If a team is way behind: Don't let them give up. Encourage them on the next question. Consider a "bonus round" at the end where trailing teams get a shot at catching up. The goal is engagement, not just winning.
