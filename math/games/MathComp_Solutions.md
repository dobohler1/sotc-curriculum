# Quarter 1: "Warm-Up Drills"

## Tier 1 — How many basketballs?

**Answer: 10**

**What students see:** A pyramid of basketballs (4 on bottom, 3, 2, 1 on top)

**Solution:**
Count the balls: 4 + 3 + 2 + 1 = 10

**If student is stuck:**
"Start at the bottom and count each row. How many on the bottom? Now the next row up?"

**Common mistakes:**
- Miscounting — have them point to each ball as they count
- Only counting one row

---

## Tier 2 — How many bags?

**Answer: 8 bags**

**What students see:** 48 basketballs, 6 per bag

**Solution:**
48 ÷ 6 = 8 bags

**If student is stuck:**
"If each bag holds 6 balls, how many groups of 6 are in 48?"
Or: "Count by 6s until you reach 48. How many times did you count?"

**Common mistakes:**
- Multiplying instead of dividing (gets 288)
- Adding 48 + 6 = 54

---

## Tier 3 — How many MORE old than new?

**Answer: 10 more old balls** (accept 9 or 10)

**What students see:** 48 basketballs, 40% are new, rest (60%) are old

**Solution:**
1. Find 40% of 48 (new balls): 48 × 0.40 = 19.2 ≈ **19 new**
2. Find 60% of 48 (old balls): 48 × 0.60 = 28.8 ≈ **29 old**
3. Difference: 29 − 19 = **10 more old**

**Shortcut:**
60% − 40% = 20% difference
48 × 0.20 = 9.6 ≈ **10**

**If student is stuck:**
"What's 10% of 48? (4.8) So what's 40%? (19.2)"

**Common mistakes:**
- Just subtracting percentages: 60 − 40 = 20 (wrong — that's not the answer)
- Forgetting to find the difference (just gives 29 or 19)
- Not knowing how to calculate percentages

## Varsity — How many players?

**Answer: 27 players**

**What students see:**
- Team has 20–35 players
- Rows of 3: 0 left over (divides evenly)
- Rows of 4: 3 left over
- Rows of 5: 2 left over

**Solution:**
Find a number between 20–35 that:
- Is divisible by 3 (no remainder)
- Leaves remainder 3 when divided by 4
- Leaves remainder 2 when divided by 5

**Step 1:** List multiples of 3 in range: 21, 24, 27, 30, 33

**Step 2:** Check each for "remainder 3 when ÷ 4":
- 21 ÷ 4 = 5 R1 ✗
- 24 ÷ 4 = 6 R0 ✗
- **27 ÷ 4 = 6 R3 ✓**
- 30 ÷ 4 = 7 R2 ✗
- 33 ÷ 4 = 8 R1 ✗

**Step 3:** Verify 27 for last condition:
- 27 ÷ 5 = 5 R2 ✓

**If student is stuck:**
"Start with numbers that divide evenly by 3. Which ones between 20 and 35 work? Now check each one against the other rules."

**Common mistakes:**
- Stopping after first condition (picks 21 or 24)
- Confusing "left over" with "divides evenly"
- Not checking all three conditions

# Quarter 2: "The Practice Court"

## Tier 1 — Total feet?

**Answer: 100 feet**

**What students see:** Player runs baseline (50 ft) 2 times

**Solution:**
50 + 50 = 100
Or: 50 × 2 = 100

**If student is stuck:**
"If you run 50 feet once, then run 50 feet again, how far did you go total?"

**Common mistakes:**
- Just writing 50 (only counting one trip)

---

## Tier 2 — Perimeter?

**Answer: 288 feet**

**What students see:** Court is 94 ft long × 50 ft wide

**Solution:**
Perimeter = all 4 sides added together
= 94 + 94 + 50 + 50
= 188 + 100
= **288 ft**

Or use formula: P = 2(length) + 2(width) = 2(94) + 2(50) = 188 + 100 = 288

**If student is stuck:**
"Perimeter means walking ALL the way around the outside. A rectangle has 4 sides — two long ones and two short ones."

**Common mistakes:**
- Calculating AREA instead (94 × 50 = 4,700)
- Only adding two sides (94 + 50 = 144)
- Adding all numbers once (94 + 50 = 144)

---

## Tier 3 — Speed in ft/sec?

**Answer: 6.4 ft/sec**

**What students see:** Player runs 288 ft in 45 seconds

**Solution:**
Speed = Distance ÷ Time
Speed = 288 ÷ 45 = **6.4 ft/sec**

**If student is stuck:**
"Speed tells you how far you go in ONE second. If she went 288 feet in 45 seconds, how do you find just one second's worth?"

**Common mistakes:**
- Multiplying instead of dividing (288 × 45)
- Dividing backwards (45 ÷ 288 = 0.156)
- Forgetting the formula

**Accept:** 6.4, 32/5, or "about 6.4"

## Varsity — When does B catch A?

**Answer: 15 seconds**

**What students see:**
- Player A runs at 6 ft/sec
- Player B runs at 8 ft/sec
- B starts 30 ft behind A
- Both start running at the same time

**Solution (Approach 1 — Relative Speed):**
1. B is faster by: 8 − 6 = 2 ft/sec
2. B gains 2 feet on A every second
3. B needs to close a 30 ft gap
4. Time = 30 ÷ 2 = **15 seconds**

**Solution (Approach 2 — Algebra):**
1. Position of A at time t: 6t
2. Position of B at time t: 8t − 30 (starts 30 ft behind)
3. B catches A when positions are equal: 6t = 8t − 30
4. Solve: 30 = 2t → t = **15 seconds**

**If student is stuck:**
"B is faster. How much faster per second? (2 ft/sec) If B gains 2 feet every second, how many seconds to gain 30 feet?"

**Common mistakes:**
- Adding speeds (gets 14)
- Dividing 30 by 8 or 6 alone (gets 3.75 or 5)
- Forgetting the 30 ft head start
- Setting up equation without the "−30"

**Bonus check:** After 15 seconds, both are at position 90 ft (A: 6×15=90, B: 8×15−30=90) ✓

# Quarter 3: "Game Stats"

## Tier 1 — How many MORE pts do Lions have?

**Answer: 6 points**

**What students see:** Scoreboard showing Lions: 24, Tigers: 18

**Solution:**
24 − 18 = **6 points**

**If student is stuck:**
"Who has more points? How do you find the difference between two numbers?"

**Common mistakes:**
- Adding instead (24 + 18 = 42)
- Subtracting wrong direction (18 − 24)

---

## Tier 2 — Total points?

**Answer: 20 points**

**What students see:**
Marcus made:
- 4 free throws (1 pt each)
- 5 two-pointers (2 pts each)
- 2 three-pointers (3 pts each)

**Solution:**
1. Free throws: 4 × 1 = 4 pts
2. Two-pointers: 5 × 2 = 10 pts
3. Three-pointers: 2 × 3 = 6 pts
4. Total: 4 + 10 + 6 = **20 points**

**If student is stuck:**
"Calculate each type of shot separately, then add them all together."

**Common mistakes:**
- Adding just the counts (4 + 5 + 2 = 11)
- Adding just the point values (1 + 2 + 3 = 6)
- Forgetting to multiply before adding

---

## Tier 3 — Shooting %?

**Answer: 60%**

**What students see:** Marcus took 20 shots, made 12

**Solution:**
Shooting % = (Made ÷ Taken) × 100
= (12 ÷ 20) × 100
= 0.60 × 100
= **60%**

**Alternate:** 12/20 = 6/10 = 60/100 = 60%

**If student is stuck:**
"What fraction of his shots did he make? (12 out of 20) Now convert that fraction to a percent."

**Common mistakes:**
- Dividing backwards (20 ÷ 12 = 1.67 or 167%)
- Subtracting (20 − 12 = 8)
- Forgetting to multiply by 100 (writes 0.6 instead of 60%)

---

## Varsity — Shots taken today?

**Answer: 12 shots**

**What students see:**
- BEFORE today: 40% shooting on 60 shots
- TODAY: Made ALL shots she took
- NOW: 50% all-time shooting percentage

**Solution:**
**Step 1:** Calculate shots made before today
40% of 60 = 0.40 × 60 = **24 makes** (out of 60 attempts)

**Step 2:** Set up equation
Let x = shots taken today (she made all of them)
- New total makes: 24 + x
- New total attempts: 60 + x
- New percentage: (24 + x)/(60 + x) = 50% = 0.50

**Step 3:** Solve
(24 + x)/(60 + x) = 0.50
24 + x = 0.50(60 + x)
24 + x = 30 + 0.5x
x − 0.5x = 30 − 24
0.5x = 6
x = **12 shots**

**Verification:**
- Total makes: 24 + 12 = 36
- Total attempts: 60 + 12 = 72
- New percentage: 36/72 = 0.50 = 50% ✓

**If student is stuck:**
"She started with 24 makes out of 60 shots. If she makes ALL her shots today, both the top and bottom of the fraction increase by the same amount. What number makes it equal 50%?"

**Common mistakes:**
- Thinking "need 10% more, so 10% of 60 = 6" (gets 6 — wrong!)
- Not realizing the denominator also increases
- Setting up (24 + x)/60 = 0.5 instead of (24 + x)/(60 + x) = 0.5
- Arithmetic errors when solving

# Quarter 4: "Championship Round"

## Tier 1 — Total medals?

**Answer: 10 medals**

**What students see:** 5 players, each gets 2 medals

**Solution:**
5 × 2 = **10 medals**

**If student is stuck:**
"If one player gets 2 medals, and there are 5 players, how many medals total?"

**Common mistakes:**
- Adding instead of multiplying (5 + 2 = 7)

---

## Tier 2 — Total GAMES played?

**Answer: 7 games**

**What students see:** Tournament bracket: 8 → 4 → 2 → 1 (champ)

**Solution:**
Count games in each round:
- Round 1: 8 teams → 4 winners = **4 games**
- Round 2: 4 teams → 2 winners = **2 games**
- Finals: 2 teams → 1 winner = **1 game**
- Total: 4 + 2 + 1 = **7 games**

**Elegant shortcut:**
In single elimination, every team except the champion loses exactly once.
8 teams − 1 champion = 7 losers = **7 games**

**If student is stuck:**
"In round 1, how many games does it take to go from 8 teams to 4 teams? (Each game eliminates one team, so 4 games.)"

**Common mistakes:**
- Counting teams instead of games (gets 8)
- Only counting the finals (gets 1)
- Counting rounds instead of games (gets 3)

## Tier 3 — Total revenue?

**Answer: $6,000**

**What students see:**
- Section A: $25 each, sold 120 tickets
- Section B: $15 each, sold 200 tickets

**Solution:**
1. Section A revenue: 25 × 120 = **$3,000**
2. Section B revenue: 15 × 200 = **$3,000**
3. Total: $3,000 + $3,000 = **$6,000**

**If student is stuck:**
"Calculate how much money Section A made first. Then Section B. Then add them together."

**Common mistakes:**
- Adding prices and tickets separately (25 + 15 = 40, 120 + 200 = 320)
- Only calculating one section (gives $3,000)
- Multiplying wrong pairs (25 × 200 or 15 × 120)

## Varsity — Each player's points?

**Answer: Alex = 28, Jordan = 14, Casey = 10**

**What students see:**
- Three players scored 52 points total
- Alex scored 2× Jordan's points
- Jordan scored Casey + 4 points

**Solution:**
**Step 1:** Define variables
Let C = Casey's points

**Step 2:** Write expressions for each player
- Casey = C
- Jordan = C + 4 (4 more than Casey)
- Alex = 2(C + 4) = 2C + 8 (twice Jordan's)

**Step 3:** Set up equation (total = 52)
C + (C + 4) + (2C + 8) = 52

**Step 4:** Solve
C + C + 4 + 2C + 8 = 52
4C + 12 = 52
4C = 40
C = **10**

**Step 5:** Find each player's points
- Casey = 10
- Jordan = 10 + 4 = **14**
- Alex = 2 × 14 = **28**

**Verification:**
- Total: 10 + 14 + 28 = 52 ✓
- Alex = 2 × Jordan: 28 = 2 × 14 ✓
- Jordan = Casey + 4: 14 = 10 + 4 ✓

**If student is stuck:**
"Pick a letter for Casey's points. Now write Jordan's points using that letter. Then Alex's. Add them all up and set equal to 52."

**Common mistakes:**
- Making Alex = 2 × Casey instead of 2 × Jordan
- Forgetting to distribute: 2(C + 4) = 2C + 4 instead of 2C + 8
- Misreading relationships (thinking Jordan = 2 × Alex)
- Arithmetic errors when combining like terms

# Tips for Verifying Student Answers

When a student claims to have the answer:

1. **Ask them to explain their reasoning** — per the rules, they must be able to explain or they lose points

2. **Quick verbal checks:**
   - "Walk me through your steps"
   - "How did you get that number?"
   - "What operation did you use?"

3. **For Varsity problems:** Students should show written work. Check that their setup is correct, not just their final answer.

4. **Partial credit guidance:**
   - Right approach, wrong arithmetic = give feedback, let them retry
   - Wrong approach entirely = point them to the key information they missed
